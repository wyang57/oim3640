My Utopia (Draft 1)

When I imagine a utopia, I picture a society that confronts challenges ethically, with systems designed to prevent harm and rehabilitate offenders, ensuring fairness. My utopia begins with the recognition that crime is often born from desperation. If people lack an essential need like housing or food, they are more likely to steal or turn to drugs. The foundation of my system is the redistribution of resources: military budgets and weapons spending are redirected to provide free housing for all families. This approach is both practical and ethical. It reflects utilitarian principles, since it maximizes happiness and reduces harm, and it represents some African humanist traditions like Teraanga ethics, which emphasize hospitality and care for others. 

Crime cannot be eliminated entirely, which is why my utopia includes two forms of punishment. The first is payment punishment, applied to crimes like theft or property damage. To avoid unfair class systems, these punishments are percentage-based. A wealthy person who steals is fined according to their revenue, just as a poor person would be fined according to theirs. The second form of punishment is harsher: permanent removal of portions of limbs for extreme crimes such as murder or severe assault. This punishment is meant to prevent further crime. A person who has committed murder would be physically limited from repeating such acts, yet they are provided simple prosthetics so they can remain contributing members of society. I chose this system because I don’t believe in the death penalty or prison. Life is both valuable and short, and spending it behind bars does not serve a purpose that benefits any party.

One of the most important principles in my utopia is the rejection of the death penalty. I do not believe death has meaning. Death subtracts life, and life is the main source of meaning. Punishment must focus on preventing future harm while preserving the present self. Deontological ethics for example, might argue that murderers deserve death, but I reject this belief. Ethics is catered toward rehabilitation and the present moment, which should support redemption, because only the living present self can change, grow, and contribute meaningfully to society. Although my system of prosthetic rehabilitation may seem violent or unjust at first thought, it is meant to embody this belief. Offenders are limited from repeating crimes but still given the chance to continue living among society and contribute.

Public disorder and corruption are also addressed, since fairness requires transparency and stability. Cameras are placed in public spaces, accessible only to residents of each location, ensuring transparency without violating privacy. Citizens can press a button to record audio if they feel threatened, preventing false accusations and protecting safety. These cameras are never installed inside private residences unless an individual has committed a corruption-related crime, such as scams or other non-physical and secretive offenses, where surveillance becomes necessary. In this way, the system ensures that public spaces remain safe while private life is respected.

Family and parenting laws are another foundation of my utopia, designed to avoid bad parenting and provide the resources parents need to succeed so the next generation can thrive. Parents are held accountable for their children’s actions, though not punished directly, instead, they receive warnings or attend supervised educational programs that teach effective parenting skills. If parents refuse guidance and children remain uncontrollable, the children can attend boarding schools which focus on community service to provide discipline and responsibility, while still allowing parents to visit under certain circumstances to maintain family bonds. This system ensures that children are raised in a positive environment where care and community support work together to strengthen society.

Education in my utopia is both conversation-based and exam-based. Instead of students advancing by grade levels, they advance by mastering individual topics. Students are free to define their own paths and are not trapped by strict structures. Mastery in this education is topic-based, meaning that individuals can reach different levels in different subjects. A student might excel in science while remaining at a lower level in math, and their progress is recorded as a profile that reflects strengths and weaknesses across disciplines. This profile can later be used in job recruitment, ensuring that people are valued for their actual skills and not penalized for areas where they struggle. The system also allows education to continue at any age, since advancement isn't tied to grade level, preventing people from being discriminated and excluded from learning later in life. Rehabilitation is also central to education. 

The job market in my utopia also reflects ethical principles. Instead of relying on networking or privilege, employment is based on simulations. Individuals train in simulated environments, and their performance determines their placement in industries. Recruitment for example, does not guarantee employment automatically. Companies can view an individual’s mastery profile and reach out to invite them to apply, but every candidate must still complete the simulation to prove their ability. This ensures that hiring remains skill-based rather than biased by connections or appearances. Pay in this system is also not fixed but fluctuates according to scarcity and demand. If there are too few doctors, their wages rise to attract more people into the profession. If a field becomes oversaturated, wages stabilize or shift toward other essential jobs to balance the market. This ensures that no single industry dominates and that society’s most necessary roles are always filled.

Health care in my utopia is structured to reflect both fairness and responsibility. Coverage is provided in two ways: through subscription and automatically based on career. More demanding or socially essential jobs, such as doctors, engineers, or mechanics, receive higher levels of insurance, while less demanding jobs provide basic coverage. This system motivates citizens to pursue careers that benefit society while ensuring that everyone has access to essential care.

Debt in my utopia is repaid through lower-class jobs that guarantee food and shelter. These positions allow individuals to work off what they owe without being punished or excluded from society. The system is designed to prevent exploitation while maintaining accountability. Workers receive essential needs but are restricted from spending on luxuries until their debt is cleared.

Serious offenders who receive prosthetics also are provided reintegration programs, ensuring they remain part of society. As part of reintegration, offenders are assigned simple community service jobs that allow them to contribute meaningfully without posing further risk. These roles often focus on environmental work, such as maintaining public spaces or supporting sustainability projects, and on assisting with the creation and distribution of prosthetics. In this way, rehabilitation is tied directly to service, ensuring that offenders both repay their debt to society and help strengthen the very systems that enabled their restoration.

Finally, in my utopia, payment exists but its purpose is redefined. Since food, housing, and healthcare are already guaranteed, currency is not tied to survival but to luxury and improvement. Money is used to enhance quality of life rather than secure basic needs. Citizens can spend earnings on better food, entertainment, furniture, or other comforts, but no one is excluded from essential resources. This system ensures fairness by removing desperation from the economy while still allowing ambition and creativity to flourish. Wealth becomes a measure of choice rather than survival, reinforcing the idea that society’s foundation must always protect dignity first.
